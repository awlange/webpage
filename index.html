Excited Electrons in DNA

The red-blue blob here is a depiction of how the three dimensional wave (or, orbital) of an electron spreads out between two adenine molecules when it is excited by ultraviolet light. The red and blue indicates the phase of the wave. The adenine dimer is somewhat hidden by the orbital, but the dimer is in the stacking geometry found in DNA and surrounded by water, serving as a model to understand electronic excitation in solvated DNA.

Fragment Molecular Orbital Multi-state Reactive Molecular Dynamics

This is a cute idea I had to combine Fragment Molecular Orbital (FMO) theory with Greg Voth's Multi-state Empirical Valence Bond (MS-EVB) theory. FMO divides a molecular system into some set of smaller fragments, but this division can be ambiguous in chemically reactive systems. MS-EVB theory deals exactly with that issue by assuming a linear combination of reactive states, like what is shown in the schematic here. Combining MS-EVB and FMO results in a new powerful model that incorporates quantum electronic structure theory into the MS-EVB approach.

Debye-Hückel-like Screening Model

This is an equation I invented/discovered to model continuum electrostatics of salty solvents characterized by a Debye screening length and a dielectric constant. I called the equation the "Debye-Hückel-like Screening Model" (DESMO) because it generalizes the classic Debye-Hückel model—a point charge centered in a spherical solute cavity surrounded by solvent continuum—to arbitrary charge densities and solute cavities. DESMO reduces to the more familiar "Conductor-like Screening Model" (COSMO) in the limit of zero salt concentration. DESMO is a simple, fast approximation to much more complicated models.

development

This is an example run of a genetic algorithm I wrote for dynamic load balancing in parallel implicit solvent calculations. (C++, MPI, OpenMP)
The molecule is a single stranded DNA polyadenine. Spheres are located at atomic coordinates and are colored by which node "owns" it. There are 4 nodes here, hence 4 colors.
The implicit solvent calculation is based on a parallel fast multipole method. The calculation is load balanced and runs faster when atoms belonging to a given node are close to each other and far away from atoms belonging to other processors. The fitness function for the genetic algorithm reflects that. The run starts with a randomized ownership of the atoms across the 4 nodes, which is essentially the worst load balance. Interestingly, the genetic algorithm finds a solution that is very close to the ideal solution for this system.
